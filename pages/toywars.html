INTRODUCTION


Toy Wars was a 3-month long project developed with Unity and C# as a part of my studies at the Academy of Interactive Entertainment (AIE). I developed this project with 6 other games development students that were also studying at AIE. This was our final assessment task of the first year, I worked with one other programmer on this project. The other programmer worked on the shooting mechanics and the soldier movement, while I worked on the turn manager, teddy bears, menus and other various scripts.

Toy Wars pits two hot-seated players against each other in a turn-based PvP combat environment. Both players have their own set of Toy Soldiers and must use them to destroy either their opponent's Soldiers or their Teddy Bear.


PROJECT GOALS


The overall goal for Toy Wars was to create a simple, turn-based, player versus player game that was inspired by worms and army men. The game was intended to be a 2 player hot seat with army soldiers and teddy bears acting as units for each player. The end goal was to kill all of the enemy units or destroy the enemy Teddy Bear before the other player did the same. The project had a few hiccups along the way but mostly turned out how we intended minus a few different weapons and unit types.

LESSONS LEARNED


During the development of Toy Wars, I learnt many things about teamwork and scope. This was one of the first group game projects most of us had ever worked on and as a result, everyone was very excited. We thought we could do anything and quickly felt the pressure of how much work we had given ourselves, so we had to scope down a lot of ideas we had dramatically.


Things I thought were simple programming tasks didn't turn out to be so simple. Things like the turn manager I had implemented very quickly at the start of development and then moved on to other tasks. I thought I was more or less done. Quickly issues started to arise and I had to rethink my strategies, the turn manager got quickly complicated after that. I leanrt how important planning and research was when it came to things like this and that I shouldn't just assume something might be simple without confirming first.
